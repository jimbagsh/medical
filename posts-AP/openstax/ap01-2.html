Chapter 1: Introduction to the Human Body
Structural Organization of the Human Body

This chapter begins with an overview of anatomy and physiology and a preview of the body regions and functions. It then covers the characteristics of life and how the body works to maintain stable conditions. It introduces a set of standard terms for body structures and for planes and positions in the body. It ends with examples of medical imaging used to see inside the living body.

Levels of Structural Organization of the Human Body

The organization of the body often is discussed in terms of six distinct levels of increasing complexity, from the smallest chemical building blocks to a unique human organism.

The Levels of Organization

All matter is made of elements. Examples are hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, nitrogen, calcium, and iron. The smallest unit of any of these elements is an atom. Atoms are made up of protons, electrons and neutrons. Two or more atoms combine to form a molecule. Examples are the water molecules, proteins, and sugars found in living things. Molecules are the chemical building blocks of all body structures.

A cell is the smallest independently functioning unit of a living organism. All living structures of anatomy contain cells. And almost all functions of human physiology are performed in cells or are started by cells.

A human cell is made of a flexible membrane that surrounds cytoplasm and organelles. Cytoplasm is a water-based fluid. And organelles are very small structures that function inside the cell. In humans, cells perform all functions of life. A tissue is a group of many similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. An organ is an anatomically structure of the body made of two or more tissue types. Each organ performs one or more specific physiological functions. An organ system is a group of organs that work together to perform major functions or meet physiological needs of the body.

This book covers eleven organ systems in the human body. Organs that “belong” to one system can also have functions in another system. In fact, most organs are part of more than one system.

Organ Systems of the Human Body

Organs that work together are grouped into organ systems.

Organ Systems of the Human Body (continued)

Organs that work together are grouped into organ systems.

The organism level is the highest level of organization. An organism is a living being that has a cellular structure and that can live. In multicellular organisms, like humans, all cells, tissues, organs, and organ systems of the body work together.

Review

The human body has several levels of structural organization. These include the chemical, cellular, tissue, organ, organ system, and the organism level. Molecules combine to form cells, cells combine to form tissues, tissues combine to form organs, organs combine to form organ systems, and organ systems combine to form organisms.

English Glossary

  • overview: a general review, summary, or survey, as of a subject or situation.
  • preview: to show or see ahead of time
  • maintain: to continue
  • stable: not easily changed
  • element: any of the 107 substances that cannot be separated into simpler substances by using chemistry
  • atom: the smallest possible unit of a chemical element
  • proton: a tiny particle in the nucleus of an atom. A proton has a positive electrical charge.
  • electron: a very small particle that moves outside the nucleus of an atom. Electrons have a negative charge.
  • neutron: a small particle present in the nucleus of all atoms except the hydrogen atom. Neutrons have no electrical charge.
  • molecule: the smallest unit of a substance that has all the properties of that substance. A molecule is made up of a single atom or group of atoms.
  • cell: a very small unit of plant or animal life, having a nucleus and surrounded by a very thin membrane.
  • flexible: easily bent without breaking.
  • membrane: a thin layer of tissue found in living things. Some kinds of membranes cover the outside or inside of organs. Other membranes separate or connect different parts of the body.
  • cytoplasm: the thick liquid substance that holds all the parts that are inside a cell.
  • organelle: a structure within a cell, such as a nucleus, that has a specialized, organlike function.
  • fluid: a liquid or gas. A fluid flows easily and takes the shape of the container that holds it. Water and air are fluids.
  • tissue: a group of cells in an animal or plant body that are like each other and do similar things.
  • organ: a part of plants or animals that performs a particular task. The heart, the lungs, the skin, and the eyes are all organs of animals
  • organism: an individual living thing, such as a plant, an animal, or a bacteria.
  • cellular: of cells
  • multicellular: made of many cells
  • structural: of a thing made up of a number of parts joined together in a certain way.
