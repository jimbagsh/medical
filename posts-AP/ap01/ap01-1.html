Anatomy & Physiology for Non-Native English Speakers

1a. Introduction

Here are some facts about the human body:

  • Your body has on the order of 10 trillion cells.
  • Every second your body is producing 15 million red blood cells.
  • Your nervous system can transmit impulses as fast as 725 kilometers per hour.
  • During an average lifetime the heart beats about 2.5 billion times.
  • In one day your blood travels nearly 1600 kilometers.

Study Tips:
Use anatomical terminology 'out loud' as often as possible. The language of anatomy is very difficult for some students and it takes a lot of of practice to feel comfortable pronouncing and using the terms. Current research proves that saying new words and phrases out loud will help you remember.

Life

How would you define a living system?

All living systems have certain characteristics. Living systems can move, grow, and respond to stimuli. They also need energy from food and oxygen from the air or water. These substances must be digested and absorbed so that they can be used for growth, maintenance and energy. Also waste products need to be excreted.

Anatomy and Physiology

Anatomy studies the structures of the body and how their relationships. Physiology studies how the body and its parts function. It includes a lot of anatomy.

Homeostasis

Homeostasis says that a system (the human body) can maintain a range of values. For example, your blood contains a sugar called glucose. Your body maintains a certain level of glucose by monitoring the glucose and then secreting certain hormones to raise or lower it. If glucose levels get too high your body responds by secreting a hormone called insulin to lower it. If glucose levels get too low then your body responds by secreting a hormone called glucagon to raise it.

Homeostasis depends on feedback mechanisms. Your body has thousands of feedback systems in place that work to regulate many substances.

There are two types of feedback:

  • Negative Feedback is when the response is opposite to the stimulus.
  • Positive feedback is where the response is the same as the stimulus.

Levels of Organization

The body is organized according to levels of complexity. The lowest level of complexity is the atom. The highest level of complexity is the organism.

Here are the levels from lowest to highest complexity:

  1. Chemical - made up of atoms and molecules
  2. Cell - basic structural and functional units of the an organism
  3. Tissue - groups of cells that work together having the same structure and function
  4. Organ - have specific functions and shape
  5. Organ System - collection of organs that work together to do one function
  6. Organism - human body

Medical Terminology

  • anatomy: the science that studies the parts and structures of plants or animals
  • homeostasis: ability of a system or living organism to adjust its internal environment to maintain a stable equilibrium
  • physiology: studies the functions and activities of life or of organs, tissues, or cells
  • terminology: words used with particular meanings

  • absorbed: to take in
  • atom: smallest possible amount of matter as a chemical element, with a nucleus surrounded by electrons.
  • cell: basic unit of a living organism, surrounded by a cell membrane
  • chemical: specific element unlike any other
  • complexity: of having many parts
  • digested: something is changed into substances that your body can use
  • excreted: to pass solid or liquid waste matter from the body
  • feedback: the effect of what happens has a connection to its cause
  • function: what something does or is used for
  • glucagon: hormone that increases the concentration of glucose and fat in the bloodstream
  • glucose: a simple sugar
  • hormones: chemical messengers of the endocrine system
  • impulses: a force or movement of energy that causes something else to react
  • insulin: hormone made by the pancreas that controls the glucose level in the blood
  • maintain: to continue the same
  • mechanisms: natural process by which something takes place or is brought about
  • molecule: smallest amount of a chemical substance that can exist
  • monitoring: watch and check the progress or quality of (something) over time
  • negative: [here] moving the in the opposite direction
  • organ: part of the body that has a specific purpose
  • organism: individual living thing, such as a plant, an animal, or a bacteria
  • positive: [here] moving in the same direction
  • range: between two numbers
  • responds: to do something because something else happened first
  • secreting: produce a fluid or other substance and release it into or out of the body
  • specific: special or unique to something
  • stimuli: {plural} something that produces a reaction
  • structures: thing made up of a number of parts joined together in a certain way
  • tissue - groups of cells that work together to do a job, having the same structure and function
  • transmit: to send an electric signal
  • trillion: 1,000,000,000,000
